High-level interests

I want to be right more often. I want to be wrong less often.

I study statistics in order to faithfully and accurately shape my decisions and opinions using data, thereby accomplishing the above goals. I am interested in all manner of applications, from business problems to the social, life, and physical sciences.

I use computers as a primary tool to accomplish this task. Through studying computer science, I have come to enjoy constructing clean, readable code to solve problems.

As I have continued to deepen my understanding of the above topics, I have developed a greater appreciation for the mathematics underlying these fields. Furthermore, I have seen its power as an educational tool.

All the while, I seek to clearly communicate what I have learned and what I believe, and I hope people enjoy and benefit from these communications.

Specific subfields of interest

  • Causal inference: gives us tools to answer the myriad interesting questions of the form, "did A cause B, and by how much"?
  • Hypothesis testing: gives a framework for drawing conclusions from data. I like to think carefully about which conclusions I can and should draw from a data analysis, and hypothesis testing frameworks guide people in doing so.
  • Information theory: gives a language for describing uncertainty.
  • Unsupervised learning: leverages computation to uncover hidden structure in data, allowing people to learn things that otherwise would have been difficult to observe.
  • Reproducibility: is more or less a necessary condition of good science or analysis. At minimum, your future self will thank you for caring about it.

Other things that have piqued my interest

  • Random number generation: uses very many interesting algorithms to do something fundamentally important that we often take for granted.
  • Copulas: solve a statistical modeling problem in a very cool way.
  • The bootstrap: is an amazingly simple and clever way to get more out of the data we have. (Nonparametric statistics in general is very interesting, and other resampling methods hold similar but lesser appeal.)
  • Missing data imputation: is a ubiquitous problem whose solutions really get at the heart of our understanding of our data and how we can analyze it properly.
  • Exploratory data analysis: is a fundamental skill that is almost never learned or taught explicitly.
  • Decision theory: seems like the theoretical framework I've been chasing my entire life, and it can even be thought of as synonymous with statistics.
